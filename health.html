Health Concerns

♦ Overview ♦

The Shih Tzus' short legs and long back are chondrodysplastic, and their short pushed-in face is brachycephalic.

Orthopedic problems

luxating patella

intervertebral disk disease

hip dysplasia

Respiratory problems

Brachycephalic Airway Syndrome

3 top concerns of elongated soft palate, stenotic nares and collapsed trachea

Breathing spasms due to reverse sneezing

Eye problems | Blindness

cataracts, progressive retinal atrophy (PRA), glaucoma

corneal ulcers, tear duct disorders

eyelash abnormalities, cherry eye, retinal dysplasia, dry eye

Other

Mammary Cancer: Benign and malignant tumors of the mammary glands occur fairly frequently in unspayed female dogs of all species.

Renal Dysplasia: A serious kidney disease that can kill Shih Tzu puppies at 6-12 months old. A simple DNA test is available, so it is easy to find out whether your dog has the disease, carries the disease, or is completely clear of it.

Urinary Stones: Shih Tzus are very prone to stones, which are especially dangerous in males.

Allergies: Can cause itchy skin and often lead to bacterial skin infections (pyoderma).

Ear Infections: Common due to profuse hair in the ear canals.

Liver Shunt: A concern in Shih Tzus, as is liver disease (chronic hepatitis).


Diseases Common to All Breeds:

easily treated conditions with available vaccinations

  • infectious diseases such as distemper and parvo
  • parasitic infecions like worms

♦ Prevention ♦

  • AT-HOME ROUTINE
    • ears: easy for parasites, bacteria and yeast to hide and thrive in
      • regular grooming/maintenance routine should include regular ear checks
      • if inner ears appear dirty, clean them with a cotton ball dampened with solution formulated specifically for this purpose (have veterinarian demonstrate proper procedures
      • do not clean so frequently or deeply as to cause irritation & NEVER insert anything
      • remove hair sprouting from canals every few weeks
    • teeth
      • regularly brush teeth
      • maintain healthy diet
      • provide with chew toys
    • easily preventable issues with veterinarian recommendations:
      • fleas and ticks can cause illnesses and not just itchiness
      • lyme disease (from ticks)
      • heartworms
  • REGULAR CHECK UPS WITH VETERINARIAN
    • vaccinations
      • core vaccines
      • noncore vaccines
      • vaccines required by law
  • MONITOR FOR IRREGULARITIES (what to watch out for)
    • eyes
      • discharge & crusty gunk
      • tearing
      • red or white eyelid linings
      • tear-stained fur
      • closed eye(s)
      • cloudiness or change in eye color
      • visible third eyelid
      • unequal pupil sizes
    • ears
      • ear discharge
      • bad smells
      • redness
      • swelling
      • crusty skin
      • hair loss
    • teeth
      • bad breath
      • excessive drooling
      • inflamed gums
      • tumors in the gums
      • cysts under the tongue
      • loose teeth

Neutering Your Male Dog

PROS

  • prevents testicular cancer | about 7% of intact males develop a testicular tumor (seldom spreads and has a cure rate over 90%, but neutering prevents it entirely)
  • reduces dominance and aggression
  • reduces the risk of dog being attacked by other males
  • helps re-focus dog's attention
  • can reduce sexual behaviors
  • keeps dog from chasing females in heat
  • reduces risk of prostate disorders | enlarged prostate occurs in 80% of intact male dogs past the age of 5
  • reduces the risk of perianal fistula painful skin disease where infected boils develop around a dog's anus
  • prevents your dog from breeding

CONS

  • triples the risk of obesity | leads to debilitating joint disease, arthritis, heart disease, pancreatitis, and diabetes
  • triples the risk of hypothyroidism
  • increases the risk of geriatric cognitive impairment
  • IF DONE AT THE WRONG AGE, neutering increases the risk of hip dysplasia, torn ligaments, and bone cancer

Spaying Your Female Dog

PROS

  • prevents the nuisance of heat periods
  • prevents deadly infections of the uterus called pyometra
  • partial protection against breast cancer | if your dog is spayed before 2-1/2 years old, she is less likely to develop mammary tumors; about half are malignant
  • prevents false pregnancy
  • prevents real pregnancy

CONS

  • doubles the risk of obesity | leads to debilitating joint disease, arthritis, heart disease, pancreatitis, and diabetes
  • increases the risk of a deadly cancer called hemangiosarcoma | spayed females are twice as likely to develop hemangiosarcoma of the spleen and five times as likely to develop hemangiosarcoma of the heart, compared to unspayed females
  • triples the risk of hypothyroidism
  • IF DONE AT THE WRONG AGE, spaying increases the risk of hip dysplasia, torn ligaments, bone cancer, and urinary incontinence
